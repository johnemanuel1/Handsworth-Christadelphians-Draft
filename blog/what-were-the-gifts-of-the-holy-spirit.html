What Were the Gifts of the Holy Spirit?

Suggested reading: 1 Corinthians 12:4–27

The New Testament describes believers speaking in other languages, healing illness and receiving inspired messages. These gifts were remarkable, but they were never intended as spiritual entertainment.

At Pentecost, the apostles received power from the Holy Spirit and spoke in languages understood by visitors from many countries. This enabled the good news about Jesus to cross barriers immediately. Similar gifts accompanied the gospel as it reached new people and places.

Signs that confirmed the message

The early Christian message made an extraordinary claim: Jesus, recently crucified, had been raised from the dead and appointed as the world's future king. God confirmed the apostles' witness through signs and wonders. Hebrews describes these gifts as God's testimony alongside those who first preached salvation.

The gifts also helped establish young communities before the New Testament was complete and widely available. Inspired teaching provided guidance. Healing showed God's compassion and offered a glimpse of the restoration promised in His Kingdom. Different languages helped the gospel spread.

Gifts for serving others

Paul corrected believers who treated dramatic gifts as signs of personal importance. In 1 Corinthians 12, he compares the community to a body whose different parts need one another. Gifts were given for the common good, not to create status.

His next chapter makes the priority unmistakable: even impressive gifts are empty without love. Patience, kindness, humility and faithfulness matter more than public display. Spiritual power should never become a reason to manipulate, impress or divide people.

Do these gifts continue today?

Christadelphians generally understand the miraculous gifts as belonging particularly to the apostolic period. They authenticated the first witnesses and helped establish the church. Paul indicated that gifts such as prophecy and tongues would cease, while faith, hope and love would remain.

This conclusion does not mean God is inactive. We believe God hears prayer, works providentially and changes lives through His word. We also recognise the many natural abilities people can use in His service. However, we do not claim the same inspired authority or miraculous powers possessed by Jesus and the apostles.

Claims of miracles deserve compassionate but careful examination. Jesus warned that impressive signs alone do not prove a message comes from God. Teaching should be tested against Scripture, and vulnerable people should never be blamed when healing does not occur.

The Spirit gifts were given to

  • confirm the apostles' witness about Jesus;
  • help the gospel cross language barriers;
  • guide and strengthen the early church;
  • serve the whole community rather than elevate individuals;
  • offer signs of the greater restoration to come.

The gifts point beyond themselves to the message they confirmed and the Kingdom they anticipated. The next articles turn to several Bible words that are often understood differently today, beginning with soul and spirit.
