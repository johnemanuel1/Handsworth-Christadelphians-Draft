What Does It Mean to Love Your Neighbour?

Suggested reading: Luke 10:25–37

Most people value kindness, especially toward family and friends. Jesus takes love further, asking us to recognise a neighbour wherever someone needs compassion and help.

When an expert in the law asked Jesus, "Who is my neighbour?", he may have hoped for a clear boundary around the command to love. Jesus answered with the parable of the Good Samaritan.

A surprising neighbour

In the story, a man is attacked, robbed and left injured beside the road. Two respected religious figures see him but pass by. A Samaritan stops, treats his wounds, carries him to safety and pays for his continuing care.

The Samaritan is a surprising hero because Jews and Samaritans generally viewed one another with suspicion and hostility. Jesus deliberately makes the outsider the person who understands God's command most clearly.

At the end, Jesus changes the original question. Instead of defining which people qualify as neighbours, he asks who acted as a neighbour. Love begins not by deciding who deserves our care, but by becoming the kind of person who shows mercy.

Love that notices and acts

The Samaritan first sees the injured man. Other people saw him too, but compassion changed what the Samaritan did next. Neighbourly love pays attention to people who are easily overlooked and allows their need to interrupt our plans.

His care is also practical. He gives time, effort, resources and follow-up support. Love is more than warm feeling or good intention. It seeks what will genuinely help another person.

Everyday opportunities may be less dramatic: listening without rushing, welcoming someone new, sharing food, offering transport, helping with a task or checking on a person who is struggling. Small actions can communicate that someone is seen and valued.

Love across boundaries

It is easier to care for people who share our background, beliefs or interests. Jesus calls his followers beyond familiar circles. A neighbour may be a stranger, a person whose choices we do not understand or someone from a community we have learned to distrust.

Love does not require agreeing with every belief or ignoring harmful behaviour. It means refusing to let difference become an excuse for indifference. Every person bears value before God and should be treated with dignity.

Neighbourly love can mean

  • noticing needs that others overlook;
  • allowing compassion to become practical help;
  • giving time and attention, not only money;
  • crossing social and cultural boundaries;
  • acting with mercy without seeking recognition.

Jesus ends with a simple instruction: "Go and do likewise." The question is not whether we can solve every need, but whether we will respond faithfully to the people placed within our reach.

Next step

Read Luke 10:25–37. Who might be easy for you to overlook, and what practical act of mercy could you offer this week?
