What Is Baptism and Why Does It Matter?

Suggested reading: Romans 6:1–11

Baptism is the Bible's appointed beginning of a committed life with Jesus. It expresses informed faith, repentance and a desire to become connected with his saving work.

In the New Testament, people first heard and understood the gospel. Those who believed responded by being baptised. Baptism was not presented as a ceremony for people who had no ability to choose, nor simply as a public badge added to an existing faith. It marked a decisive response to God's invitation.

Belief before baptism

Jesus told his followers to make disciples, baptising and teaching them. In Acts, people ask questions, receive the message and then choose baptism. Understanding does not need to be perfect or exhaustive, but the person should know the gospel they are accepting and the commitment they are making.

Baptism is closely linked with repentance. To repent is to change our mind and direction: recognising sin, accepting God's way as right and turning toward Him. Baptism does not claim that the person has become perfect. It acknowledges their need for forgiveness and their intention to begin again.

Buried and raised with Christ

The Greek word translated baptism carries the sense of dipping or immersing. Christadelphians therefore practise baptism by fully immersing a believing person in water.

Romans 6 explains the meaning. Going beneath the water represents sharing in Jesus' death and burial. Rising from the water represents sharing in his resurrection and beginning a new life. The action tells the gospel story personally and powerfully.

Baptism connects a believer with Jesus and with God's promises. Paul describes those baptised into Christ as belonging to him and becoming heirs of the promise made to Abraham. It is the beginning of a relationship shaped by grace, trust and discipleship.

Can water save someone?

There is nothing magical about water. Baptism without belief or repentance is only getting wet. Salvation comes from God through Jesus Christ, not from human achievement.

Yet dismissing baptism because it cannot earn salvation would also miss the Bible's teaching. Faith responds to God's grace in the way He asks. Baptism matters because Jesus commanded it and because God has given it meaning.

Baptism expresses

  • belief in the gospel of Jesus and God's Kingdom;
  • repentance and the desire for forgiveness;
  • identification with Jesus' death and resurrection;
  • entry into Christ and God's covenant promises;
  • the beginning of a new life of discipleship.

A beginning, not a graduation

Baptism follows learning, but it does not mark the end of learning. The newly baptised believer still grows, struggles, receives forgiveness and depends upon the support of others.
