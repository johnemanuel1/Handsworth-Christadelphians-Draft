Why Do Christians Break Bread Together?

Suggested reading: 1 Corinthians 11:23–29

Before his death, Jesus shared bread and wine with his disciples and asked them to remember him. Christian communities have continued this simple practice ever since.

Different traditions use names such as communion, the Lord's Supper or breaking bread. Christadelphians commonly speak of the Breaking of Bread or Memorial Service and usually share it each Sunday.

Remembering Jesus

At the final meal with his disciples, Jesus gave the bread and wine a special meaning. The bread pointed to his body, given in faithful service. The wine pointed to his blood, poured out as the new covenant was established through his sacrifice.

Breaking bread slows believers down and brings them back to the centre of their faith. It recalls Jesus' love, obedience, suffering and victory. It is not a repetition of his sacrifice, which was offered once, but a thankful remembrance of what God accomplished through him.

Examining ourselves

Paul encourages believers to examine themselves before eating and drinking. This is not an invitation to decide whether we have become worthy through our own goodness. Nobody approaches Jesus on that basis. It is an opportunity for honesty, repentance and renewed gratitude for grace.

Remembering Jesus exposes pride, resentment and indifference. It also reassures those conscious of failure that forgiveness is available. The symbols point away from self-reliance and toward God's mercy.

Sharing one fellowship

Breaking bread is personal, but never merely private. Believers share one loaf as members of one body. The meal challenges divisions and calls the community to practical care.

Paul strongly criticised a church that observed the meal while humiliating poorer members. Their behaviour contradicted its meaning. Remembering the self-giving love of Jesus should shape how Christians treat one another before, during and after worship.

Looking forward

The meal remembers the past and anticipates the future. Paul says believers proclaim the Lord's death "until he comes". Jesus also spoke of sharing the meal anew in God's Kingdom.

Breaking bread therefore gathers the gospel into a simple action: Jesus gave himself, God raised him, a forgiven community is being formed, and Jesus will return. Week by week, believers renew their commitment to live in the light of that hope.

Breaking bread helps believers

  • remember Jesus' faithful life and sacrifice;
  • receive God's grace with humility;
  • examine attitudes and choices honestly;
  • recognise their responsibility to one another;
  • look forward to Jesus' return and God's Kingdom.

At Handsworth, baptised believers share bread and wine during the Sunday Memorial Service, while visitors are warmly welcome to attend and observe. The meeting also includes Bible readings, prayer, hymns and an encouraging reflection from Scripture.

This pathway has explored subjects that often raise further questions. The best next step is not simply to remember the conclusions, but to return to the suggested passages, examine their contexts and continue the conversation.
