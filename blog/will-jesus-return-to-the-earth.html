Will Jesus Return to the Earth?

Suggested reading: Acts 1:6–11

The return of Jesus is not a minor detail added to Christian belief. It is the event through which the Bible's promises of resurrection, judgement and God's Kingdom move toward fulfilment.

After his resurrection, Jesus spent time teaching his disciples about the Kingdom of God. Acts 1 then describes him being taken from their sight. Two angels assured the disciples that this same Jesus would return in the same way they had seen him go.

A personal and visible return

The New Testament does not describe the return of Jesus merely as an idea, a private spiritual experience or the gradual improvement of society. It speaks of the risen Jesus returning personally and openly.

This matters because the world's deepest problems require more than inspiration. War, injustice, oppression, suffering and death continue despite remarkable human achievement. The Bible's hope is that God will intervene through the king He has appointed.

Why will Jesus return?

Jesus returns to establish the Kingdom of God, raise the dead, judge with righteousness and begin the promised renewal of the earth. The prophets describe nations learning peace, the vulnerable receiving justice and the knowledge of God filling the world.

His return also completes the work begun through his first coming. Jesus has already died and been raised, demonstrating God's victory over sin and death. His return extends that victory, bringing resurrection and life to those who belong to him.

When will it happen?

Jesus taught that no one knows the exact day or hour. Christians have sometimes made confident predictions, but the Bible discourages date-setting. It instead calls people to remain watchful, faithful and ready.

Bible passages describe signs of a troubled world and the continuing importance of Israel, but these are intended to encourage alertness rather than speculation. The practical question is not whether we can create a timetable, but whether the promised return of Jesus shapes our priorities.

The return of Jesus means

  • history is moving toward God's purpose rather than endless chaos;
  • injustice and suffering will not have the final word;
  • resurrection and judgement are real future events;
  • God's Kingdom will be established on earth;
  • faithfulness today has lasting significance.

A hope that changes the present

Waiting for Jesus is not passive. His followers are called to practise the values of the coming Kingdom now: mercy, truth, peace, justice and love. They know their efforts cannot establish God's Kingdom by human power, but they can live as people who genuinely expect it.

The return of Jesus therefore offers both comfort and challenge. It comforts us because God will put the world right. It challenges us because our lives matter to the returning king.

Next step

Read Acts 1:6–11. What were the disciples discussing, and what were they told about the manner of Jesus' return?
