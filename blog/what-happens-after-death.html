What Happens After Death?

Suggested reading: Ecclesiastes 9:4–10

Questions about death are deeply personal. We naturally wonder whether life continues, where those we love have gone and what hope the Bible offers.

Many people assume Christianity teaches that every person possesses an immortal soul which leaves the body at death and immediately goes to heaven or hell. Christadelphians believe the Bible presents a different, and ultimately more hopeful, picture.

Human beings are mortal

In Genesis, the first human is formed from the dust and becomes a living being when God gives breath. The person does not receive a separate immortal soul; the whole person is alive through God's gift. When that life ends, the person returns to the dust.

Throughout the Bible, death is described as an enemy and often compared to sleep. Ecclesiastes speaks plainly about the dead no longer participating in the activity and awareness of ordinary life. The Psalms similarly describe death as silence rather than conscious existence elsewhere.

This can sound stark, but it takes the reality of death seriously. The Bible does not call death a doorway naturally overcome by something immortal within us. Life belongs to God, and continued life depends upon His saving action.

The hope is resurrection

Because the dead are truly dead, resurrection becomes essential. God remembers those who have died and is able to restore them to life. Jesus spoke of a future time when the dead would hear his voice and come out of their graves.

The resurrection of Jesus provides the foundation for this hope. God did not merely release an immortal part of Jesus; He raised Jesus from death to life. The same God promises to raise those who belong to Christ when Jesus returns.

What about heaven?

The Bible describes heaven as God's dwelling place and teaches that Jesus is presently there at God's right hand. Its consistent hope for believers, however, is resurrection and life in God's Kingdom when Jesus returns to the earth.

This places the future within the Bible's wider purpose: not abandoning creation, but renewing it. Eternal life is God's gift, not something human beings automatically possess.

The Bible's pattern

  • God gives life; human beings are mortal.
  • Death is an unconscious state often compared to sleep.
  • God remembers the dead and can restore life.
  • Jesus was raised and will return.
  • The believer's hope is resurrection and life in God's Kingdom.

Hope grounded in God

This teaching does not remove grief. Jesus himself wept at the grave of his friend Lazarus. But it directs hope away from an assumed immortality and toward the faithful God who raises the dead.
