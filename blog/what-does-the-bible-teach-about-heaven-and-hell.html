What Does the Bible Teach About Heaven and Hell?

Suggested reading: Acts 2:22–36

Many people assume Christianity teaches that good souls go to heaven and bad souls suffer forever in hell. Christadelphians believe the Bible's central hope is different: resurrection and life in God's Kingdom on earth.

Heaven and hell are certainly Bible words, but their meaning should come from the passages where they appear rather than from popular images.

What is heaven?

Heaven is God's dwelling place and the source of His authority. Jesus is now in heaven at the Father's right hand, and believers are encouraged to set their minds on heavenly things. Yet the Bible does not generally present heaven as the final home of human beings.

Jesus taught his followers to pray for God's Kingdom to come and His will to be done "on earth as it is in heaven". The prophets promised peace and justice on earth. Jesus promised that the meek would inherit the earth, and the apostles looked forward to his return and the resurrection of the dead.

Acts 2 makes an interesting distinction. David, despite being a faithful man, had not ascended to heaven. He remained dead while awaiting the fulfilment of God's promise through Jesus. The Christian hope rests upon resurrection, not the natural journey of an immortal soul.

What does hell mean?

Several different Bible words are translated as hell. The Hebrew Sheol and Greek Hades usually refer to the grave or the realm of the dead. They can describe the destination of all who die, not a place where conscious souls are tortured.

Jesus also used the word Gehenna, the name of a valley outside Jerusalem associated with judgment and destruction. Its powerful imagery warns that rejecting God has serious consequences. Another term, translated "hell" in one New Testament passage, describes sinful messengers being restrained for judgment. Christadelphians understand these to be human messengers, not fallen heavenly angels.

These words differ, so combining every occurrence into one picture can be misleading. Christadelphians do not believe the Bible teaches eternal conscious torment. The consistent consequence of sin is death, while eternal life is God's gift.

Judgment still matters

Rejecting eternal torment does not make human choices unimportant. God is just, and the Bible teaches a coming judgment. Some will receive eternal life; others will face rejection and final death. Jesus' warnings are serious because life in God's Kingdom is precious and should not be treated casually.

The Bible's larger pattern

  • Heaven is God's dwelling place and Jesus is presently there.
  • Jesus will return from heaven to the earth.
  • The dead await resurrection rather than living naturally elsewhere.
  • Bible words translated hell must be read in their contexts.
  • God offers eternal life; the final consequence of sin is death.

This understanding replaces frightening speculation with a serious but hopeful message. God does not delight in suffering. He invites people to receive life through Jesus and share in a renewed world.
