Can We Trust the Bible?

Suggested reading: Luke 1:1–4

The Bible asks readers to take its message seriously. It is therefore reasonable to ask whether it deserves our trust rather than accepting it without thought.

This question has several parts. Has the text been preserved? Are its writers concerned with real events? Does its message remain coherent? And does reading it give us reason to believe its claim to speak for God?

Has the text survived?

The Bible was copied by hand for centuries before printing. That may sound unreliable, but large numbers of surviving manuscripts allow scholars to compare copies and identify variations. Some differences exist, mostly involving spelling or word order, but they do not leave us guessing about the Bible's central message.

Modern translations are made from Hebrew, Aramaic and Greek manuscripts rather than from a long chain of translations. Differences between translations can usually be explored through footnotes or comparison, making the process more open rather than less trustworthy.

Are the writers interested in truth?

Bible writers frequently place events within identifiable locations, rulers and customs. Luke begins his Gospel by explaining that he investigated earlier accounts and intended to write an orderly record so his reader could know the certainty of what had been taught.

The writers also describe the failures of their own people and heroes. Abraham, Moses, David, Peter and other important figures are not presented as flawless legends. Their fear, sin and misunderstanding remain in the story. This uncomfortable honesty is one reason the Bible often feels unlike propaganda.

Archaeology and historical research cannot prove every Bible claim, but they repeatedly illuminate its world. Ancient places, inscriptions and customs provide valuable points of contact between the text and history.

A message developed across centuries

As we saw in the previous article, the Bible is a collection of books written in different settings. Yet its central themes develop with remarkable continuity: one Creator, human failure, promises of blessing, a coming king, sacrifice, resurrection and God's Kingdom.

Christians also point to fulfilled prophecy and, above all, the resurrection of Jesus. These are significant claims worth examining carefully, not shortcuts that remove every question.

A thoughtful way to investigate

  • Read complete passages rather than isolated quotations.
  • Ask what the writer intended to communicate.
  • Compare claims with history and other Bible passages.
  • Distinguish genuine difficulties from assumptions about what the Bible says.
  • Remain open to being challenged as well as reassured.

Trust often grows through patient investigation. The Bible does not need to be protected from honest questions. Its invitation is to read, examine and consider whether its explanation of God, humanity, Jesus and hope rings true.

You may not settle every question quickly. A useful first step is simply to approach the text fairly and allow it to make its own case.

It is also worth asking what kind of evidence would count. The Bible addresses history, but it also makes moral and spiritual claims that must be considered through sustained reading. Its unity, realism, fulfilled hope and effect upon readers form a cumulative case rather than one isolated proof.
