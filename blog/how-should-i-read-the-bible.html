How Should I Read the Bible?

Suggested reading: Acts 17:10–12

Reading the Bible well does not require specialist training, but it does benefit from patience, good questions and a willingness to listen before reaching conclusions.

The Bible is sometimes opened at random for a quick answer. Occasionally a single verse may encourage us, but reading only isolated lines can easily hide what a writer meant. A better approach is to treat each passage as part of a conversation, book and larger story.

Begin with context

Ask simple questions. Who is speaking or writing? Who first received these words? What has happened immediately before? Is the passage history, poetry, prophecy, a parable or a letter?

Genre matters. Poetry uses imagery. Proverbs describe wise patterns rather than unconditional promises. Parables invite reflection. Letters often respond to particular questions or problems. Reading each kind of writing appropriately prevents confusion.

Observe before interpreting

Notice repeated words, contrasts, questions, promises and surprising details. Try summarising the passage in one sentence before deciding what it means for you. Often the text answers questions we had not thought to ask.

Then compare the passage with other relevant parts of the Bible. Clear passages can help explain difficult ones, and recurring themes reveal connections. Cross-references are useful, but always return to the original context.

Acts 17 describes people in Berea who received the message eagerly while examining the Scriptures daily to see whether it was true. Their example combines openness with careful thought. They neither rejected the message immediately nor accepted it uncritically.

A simple reading pattern

  • Read: take a manageable section slowly, perhaps twice.
  • Observe: note what the passage actually says.
  • Connect: place it within the book and the Bible's wider story.
  • Question: record anything unclear or challenging.
  • Respond: consider what it teaches about God, Jesus, hope or faithful living.

Build a sustainable habit

A small amount read regularly is usually more valuable than an ambitious plan quickly abandoned. Ten or fifteen minutes is enough to begin. Choose a consistent time, keep a notebook nearby and do not be discouraged by difficult sections.

Helpful tools include a modern translation, maps, cross-references and a Bible dictionary. Discussion with others can expose assumptions and offer new insight. However, tools and teachers should help us examine the Bible, not replace reading it.

Approach the Bible with curiosity and humility. Expect to learn, but also expect to be challenged. Its purpose is not merely to provide information; it invites us to know God, trust His promises and allow His word to shape our lives.

Questions are part of good reading, not evidence that you are failing. Write them down and return to them later. Sometimes another chapter supplies the answer; sometimes a conversation or reference tool helps; and sometimes a question remains open while your wider understanding grows.

Prayer can accompany this process. Asking God for wisdom does not replace careful reading, but it reminds us why we are reading. The goal is not simply to win arguments or collect facts. It is to hear honestly, understand responsibly and respond faithfully.

Next step

Read Acts 17:10–12 using the five-step pattern above. What do the Bereans teach us about an open and careful approach?
