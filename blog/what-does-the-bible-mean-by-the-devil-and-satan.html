What Does the Bible Mean by the Devil and Satan?

Suggested reading: James 1:12–18

Images of a supernatural devil have shaped art, stories and popular religion for centuries. Christadelphians believe the Bible directs attention closer to home: toward the power of sin and the desires that tempt human beings.

The words themselves are helpful. Satan means adversary or opponent. Devil means false accuser or slanderer. Depending on the context, these terms can describe people, authorities or the personified power of sin.

Adversaries and accusers

In the Old Testament, the Hebrew word for satan is sometimes used of ordinary human opponents. On another occasion, an angel acting on God's instructions stands as an adversary in someone's path. The word does not automatically identify a rebellious supernatural being.

In the New Testament, Jesus even calls Peter "Satan" when Peter opposes the path God has set before him. Peter had not become a supernatural creature; he was thinking as an adversary to God's purpose. Elsewhere, hostile governments and persecuting powers are described using symbolic language associated with the devil.

Where temptation begins

James gives a direct explanation of temptation. Each person is tempted when drawn away by their own desire. Desire, when allowed to grow, produces sin, and sin leads to death. Responsibility is not transferred to an external monster.

Jesus taught that evil thoughts and actions come from within the human heart. Paul similarly describes sin as a power operating within human nature. The Bible can personify this power, speaking of sin ruling, deceiving or being defeated, just as it personifies wisdom and death.

Christadelphians therefore understand the devil in its broadest Bible sense as the personification of sin, expressed through individuals and systems that oppose God. This makes evil no less real. It makes the struggle deeply personal and calls us to honest responsibility.

What about dramatic passages?

Passages such as Jesus' temptation, the serpent in Eden and the dragon in Revelation deserve careful reading in their own literary settings. Revelation openly communicates through symbols. Genesis presents a serpent described as one of the creatures God made. The Gospel temptations reveal real pressure upon Jesus, who overcame every invitation to misuse his position.

No single passage should be isolated from the Bible's repeated teaching that God is supreme, angels faithfully serve Him, and human desire gives birth to sin.

This understanding encourages us to

  • take responsibility for our choices;
  • recognise how sin works within people and societies;
  • resist false accusation and opposition to God;
  • look to Jesus, who overcame temptation;
  • trust that God will finally remove sin and death.

The gospel announces that Jesus shared human nature yet defeated sin through faithful obedience. His victory offers forgiveness now and the hope that evil will ultimately be removed from God's creation.
