Why Did Jesus Have to Die?

Suggested reading: Romans 5:6–11

Jesus' death can be difficult to understand. Why would the person at the centre of God's good news be rejected and crucified? Why could God not simply overlook human wrongdoing?

The Bible presents the cross not as an unexpected defeat, but as the point where human sin, God's righteousness and extraordinary love are brought into the open.

The human problem revealed

Sin is more than isolated mistakes. It is the persistent human tendency to distrust God and place our own desires first. It damages relationships, produces injustice and leads eventually to death. The Bible does not treat this problem lightly because God does not treat either evil or its victims lightly.

Jesus entered fully into mortal human life. He experienced weakness and temptation but consistently chose his Father's will. In him we see human nature confronted and overcome through faithful obedience.

A willing sacrifice

Jesus' death was an act of willing love. He did not seek suffering for its own sake, nor was he an unwilling victim of an angry God. Father and Son were united in the work of salvation. God gave His Son, and Jesus freely gave himself.

The cross declares that sin deserves judgement while also opening a way for sinners to be forgiven. Jesus represented those he came to save. He shared their mortality, resisted sin and carried obedience all the way to death. God then raised him, demonstrating that sin and death had been overcome in him.

Christadelphians understand the cross as something believers are invited to identify with, not merely a punishment transferred from the guilty to an unrelated innocent person. God's forgiveness is freely given, but it calls us to recognise His judgement on sin, turn from it and become united with Jesus.

Reconciliation, not reluctant forgiveness

Romans 5 says that God demonstrates His love through Christ's death while we were still sinners. The cross does not persuade an unwilling God to love people. It reveals the love already moving God to rescue and reconcile them.

Reconciliation means a damaged relationship being restored. Through Jesus, God invites people who were distant to approach Him with confidence, receive forgiveness and begin a changed life.

The cross shows us

  • the seriousness and destructive power of sin;
  • the righteousness and mercy of God;
  • the faithful obedience of Jesus;
  • God's desire to forgive and reconcile;
  • the pattern of self-giving love believers are called to follow.

The cross cannot be separated from the resurrection. If Jesus had remained dead, the story would end only with sacrifice. God raised him to immortal life, confirming his faithfulness and opening hope for others. The next article explores why that resurrection changes everything.
